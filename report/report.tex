\begin{abstract}
Surrogate-assisted multi-objective optimization (MOO) trains a machine learning
model on a small evaluated subset to predict objectives for the remaining
configurations, then identifies the Pareto front from predictions.
The choice of \emph{how} to select that subset---the sampling strategy---is
critical yet understudied, especially when the evaluation budget is a fixed
absolute count rather than a percentage of dataset size.
We present the largest empirical comparison of fixed-budget sampling strategies
for surrogate-assisted MOO: \textbf{50 tabular datasets} (93--20,000 rows;
2--8 objectives), \textbf{4 strategies} (Random, Stratified, Clustering,
Diversity/MaxMin), \textbf{4 budget levels} ($n \in \{50, 100, 200, 250\}$),
and 10 repeats per configuration---\textbf{7,640 total experiments}.
Our key finding is a sharp, reproducible crossover on the primary metric
(Pareto Recall):
\textbf{Clustering recovers the most true Pareto points at $n=50$}
(28/50 dataset wins by median recall, median recall 0.093), while
\textbf{Diversity dominates at $n \geq 100$}, with median recall climbing to
0.275 at $n=250$~(42\% above Clustering, 54\% above Random).
On the secondary metrics (Precision, IGD, HV-diff), Diversity is the best
strategy on Precision and HV-diff at every budget, and on IGD from $n=100$
onward (only Stratified marginally beats it on IGD at $n=50$:
0.084 vs.\ 0.089).
The two views agree from $n=100$ upward; at $n=50$ they reflect a classic
recall--precision trade-off (Clustering predicts a wider front---more hits
but more noise).
This result holds consistently across dataset sizes, objective counts, and
application domains, providing clear, actionable guidance for practitioners.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Many software engineering tasks require \emph{evaluating} configurations to
assess quality---compiling a configurable system, running a test suite under
different build flags, simulating a project schedule, or measuring service
latency under varying resource settings.
Each evaluation is expensive, and when multiple objectives must be balanced
simultaneously, the practitioner needs to approximate the \emph{Pareto front}
without evaluating every configuration.
Surrogate-assisted multi-objective optimization (MOO) addresses this by
training a cheap predictive model on a small evaluated subset, then predicting
objectives for the remainder~\cite{wang2020adaptive,nair2018finding}.
The quality of the predicted front depends critically on \emph{which}
configurations are sampled, yet sampling strategy is treated as an afterthought
in most surrogate-assisted MOO pipelines.

A second methodological issue is how budgets are framed.
An earlier version of this work used \emph{percentage-based} budgets
(5\%--30\% of each dataset), and several performance-prediction studies for
configurable systems also report budgets as fractions of the valid
configurations~\cite{siegmund2012predicting,guo2018data}.
Percentage framing conflates evaluation cost with dataset size: 5\% is
5~evaluations on a 100-row space but 1{,}000 on a 20{,}000-row space.
In practice, a practitioner's constraint is \emph{absolute}; they can afford
$n$ evaluations regardless of space size.
As we show in Section~\ref{sec:gap}, no prior work addresses sampling-strategy
choice for surrogate-assisted MOO under fixed absolute budgets on a large,
diverse SE benchmark.

This paper fills that gap.
We compare \textbf{4~strategies} (Random, Stratified, Clustering,
Diversity/MaxMin) on \textbf{50~tabular datasets} (93--20{,}000~rows;
2--8~objectives; SE process models, configuration spaces, and general tabular
domains) at \textbf{4~fixed budgets} ($n\in\{50,100,200,250\}$) with
\textbf{10~repeats} each---\textbf{7{,}640~experiments}.
We find a sharp, reproducible budget-dependent crossover on the primary
recall metric: Clustering is best at $n=50$ (28/50~dataset wins by median
recall; median recall~0.093), Diversity takes over at $n\geq 100$ (reaching
0.275 at $n=250$, 42\% above Clustering and 54\% above Random).
On the three secondary metrics (Precision, IGD, HV-diff), Diversity is in
fact the best strategy at nearly every budget---only Stratified marginally
beats it on IGD at $n=50$ (0.084 vs.\ 0.089), and on Precision and HV-diff
Diversity dominates everywhere.
To our knowledge, this is the first crossover result reported for sampling
strategies in surrogate-assisted MOO, and the fixed-budget framing is what
makes it visible.

\subsection{Research Questions}

\begin{description}
  \item[\textbf{RQ1}] \textit{Which sampling strategy most effectively recovers
  the Pareto front under a fixed evaluation budget?}

  \item[\textbf{RQ2}] \textit{Does the optimal sampling strategy change as the
  fixed budget increases?}
\end{description}

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Gap analysis}: a Venn-diagram review of 17 high-citation
  papers shows that no prior work simultaneously satisfies all five criteria
  that define our setting (Section~\ref{sec:gap}).
  \item \textbf{Scale}: the largest fixed-budget sampling study for
  surrogate-assisted MOO---50 datasets, 4 strategies, 4 budgets, 10 repeats
  (7{,}640 experiments).
  \item \textbf{Novel finding}: a sharp Clustering-to-Diversity crossover at
  $n\approx 100$ on Pareto Recall, while Diversity dominates Precision, IGD,
  and HV-diff at nearly every budget---the first such crossover reported
  for sampling strategies in surrogate-assisted MOO.
  \item \textbf{Actionable guidance}: a budget-only decision rule
  (\emph{Clustering $\leq 50$; Diversity $\geq 100$}) usable before any data
  are collected.
\end{itemize}

\section{A Gap in the Literature}
\label{sec:gap}

To establish that the problem tackled in this paper is genuinely open, we
conducted a structured literature review.
Following the knee-set methodology of Senthilkumar and
Menzies~\cite{senthilkumar2025llm}, we assembled a candidate pool of
60+~papers drawn from the MOOT repository's citation network, the Semantic
Scholar API, and domain literature on surrogate-assisted optimization and
software configuration.
Papers were then ranked by citation count, and the \emph{knee} of the citation
curve---the point where citation accumulation drops sharply---was used as a
threshold separating core contributions from long-tail work.
Snowballing from the knee set added closely related papers that fell just
below the threshold.
This procedure yielded \textbf{17 representative papers} that together
cover the prior work most likely to overlap with our contribution.

\subsection{Five Criteria Defining Our Setting}

Each of the 17 papers was checked against the five criteria that together
define the problem addressed in this paper:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Compares Sampling Strategies}: evaluates multiple ways of
  choosing a training subset head-to-head.
  \item \textbf{Surrogate Model}: trains a cheap predictive model on the
  sampled subset to predict objectives for unevaluated configurations.
  \item \textbf{Multi-Objective}: handles two or more objectives and computes
  a Pareto front (rather than a single scalar objective).
  \item \textbf{Tabular SE Data}: evaluates on real software-engineering
  benchmarks rather than synthetic continuous functions.
  \item \textbf{Fixed-Budget Aware}: varies an \emph{absolute} evaluation
  budget $n$ (rather than a fraction of dataset size).
\end{enumerate}

           that define our setting. The centre intersection---where all five

\subsection{Observations}

Figure~\ref{fig:venn} visualises the coverage.
The five-way intersection at the centre of the diagram is \emph{empty}: no
paper in the knee set satisfies all five criteria simultaneously.
The individual criteria are unevenly covered:

\paragraph{Compares Sampling Strategies? (3 of 17).}
Bergstra and Bengio~\cite{bergstra2012random} compare random vs.\ grid search,
but for single-objective hyperparameter optimization on continuous spaces.
Jamshidi et al.~\cite{jamshidi2018learning} explore transfer learning across
environments but do not run head-to-head strategy comparisons.
Chen et al.~\cite{chen2019sampling} compare SWAY against evolutionary
algorithms but do not evaluate which sampling strategy produces the best
surrogate---SWAY itself reads all objective values to build its splitting
tree, effectively a 100\% budget.

\paragraph{Surrogate Model? (9 of 17).}
About half the knee set uses surrogates~\cite{nair2018finding,wang2020adaptive,zuluaga2013active,siegmund2012predicting,guo2018data,jamshidi2018learning,jin2003evolutionary,ha2019deepperf,valov2017transferring},
but overwhelmingly for \emph{single-objective} configuration tasks or
continuous engineering benchmarks.
None systematically study \emph{how to choose} the training data for the
surrogate.

\paragraph{Multi-Objective? (10 of 17).}
Most papers in the knee set handle MOO~\cite{chen2019sampling,sayyad2013value,nair2018finding,wang2020adaptive,zuluaga2013active,jin2003evolutionary,sarro2017adaptive,krall2015gale,deb2002fast,senthilkumar2025llm},
but typically either without a surrogate (evolutionary/exhaustive search)
or without real SE benchmarks (DTLZ/ZDT style continuous problems).

\paragraph{Tabular SE Data? (13 of 17).}
SE benchmarks dominate the corpus~\cite{chen2019sampling,sayyad2013value,nair2018finding,jamshidi2018learning,siegmund2012predicting,guo2018data,sarro2017adaptive,krall2015gale,ha2019deepperf,kaltenecker2020distance,sarkar2015cost,valov2017transferring,oh2017finding}---this
is expected given the MOOT-centred seed---yet only 3 of these also use a
surrogate for a multi-objective task.

\paragraph{Fixed-Budget Aware? (6 of 17).}
Six papers vary an evaluation budget~\cite{bergstra2012random,siegmund2012predicting,guo2018data,nair2018finding,zuluaga2013active,sarkar2015cost},
but \emph{none} of them also compare multiple sampling strategies.
Several of these frame the budget as a fraction of the configuration
space~\cite{siegmund2012predicting,guo2018data,sarkar2015cost} rather than
as an absolute count, which, as our results show
(Section~\ref{sec:results}), masks the budget-dependent crossover between
strategies.

\subsection{What This Paper Adds}

Unlike any single prior work in the knee set, this paper simultaneously
(a)~compares 4~sampling strategies head-to-head (Random, Stratified,
Clustering, Diversity/MaxMin);
(b)~trains a Random Forest surrogate on the sampled data;
(c)~handles multi-objective optimization with 2--8 objectives;
(d)~evaluates on 50~real tabular benchmarks, dominated by SE datasets from
MOOT but including cross-domain general tabular data;
and (e)~varies an \emph{absolute} evaluation budget ($n\in\{50,100,200,250\}$)
rather than a dataset-size percentage.
The empty centre of Figure~\ref{fig:venn} is exactly the region that this
paper occupies.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

This section expands on the gap analysis in Section~\ref{sec:gap} by
describing the technical context (the surrogate-assisted MOO pipeline and
the four families of sampling strategy it relies on) and positioning the
17~knee-set papers from Figure~\ref{fig:venn} in detail.

\subsection{The Surrogate-Assisted MOO Pipeline}

Evaluating all configurations in a large space is infeasible when each
evaluation is expensive---e.g., compiling a configurable software system,
running an integration test suite, simulating a project schedule, or
benchmarking a deployed service under different resource settings.
Surrogate-assisted optimization~\cite{wang2020adaptive} breaks this bottleneck
by training a cheap predictive model on a small evaluated subset and using it
to predict the objectives of the remaining, unevaluated
configurations~\cite{nair2018finding}.
The Pareto front of the merged (predicted~+~measured) objective matrix is
then returned as the optimisation output.

Three design choices drive the quality of this pipeline:
(i)~the \emph{surrogate family} (e.g., Random Forest, Gaussian Process,
neural network);
(ii)~the \emph{evaluation budget} (how many configurations are actually
run); and
(iii)~the \emph{sampling strategy} (which specific configurations the budget
is spent on).
Prior work has extensively compared surrogate families and varied budgets,
but sampling strategy---the third factor---is typically fixed at ``random'' by
default and rarely compared head-to-head in a multi-objective setting
(see Figure~\ref{fig:venn}).

\subsection{Four Families of Sampling Strategy}

From the knee set, four broad families of sampling strategy emerge.
Our experiments compare one representative of each.

\paragraph{Random sampling.}
Bergstra and Bengio~\cite{bergstra2012random} famously showed that random
search beats grid search for single-objective hyperparameter optimisation on
continuous spaces.
Random sampling remains the dominant default in surrogate-assisted MOO
papers~\cite{nair2018finding,wang2020adaptive} because it is unbiased and
trivially implementable.
Its weakness is that on small budgets it produces clumpy coverage: two of
fifty drawn points can land arbitrarily close together, wasting an evaluation.

\paragraph{Stratified sampling.}
Stratification along one or more axes---typically the primary
objective---ensures coverage of the objective \emph{range}.
Siegmund et al.~\cite{siegmund2012predicting} and Guo et
al.~\cite{guo2018data} apply variants of this for single-objective
performance prediction of configurable systems.
No prior work in the knee set evaluates stratification as a primary strategy
for multi-objective Pareto-front recovery, despite its popularity in
supervised-learning pipelines.

\paragraph{Clustering-based sampling.}
Space-filling designs via $k$-Means or related clustering methods are common
in surrogate modelling for computer experiments~\cite{zuluaga2013active}, but
are typically studied on \emph{continuous} engineering benchmarks with
Gaussian-process surrogates.
Their behaviour on tabular SE benchmarks under tight fixed budgets, and with
a Random Forest surrogate, has not been reported.

\paragraph{Diversity-based sampling.}
Greedy max-min (MaxMin) selection---start from a seed point, then
iteratively pick the point that maximises the minimum distance to the
already-selected set~\cite{sayyad2013value}---is the workhorse behind
SWAY~\cite{chen2019sampling} and related diversity-driven search procedures.
A critical caveat is that SWAY reads \emph{all} rows' objective values to
build its splitting tree, effectively requiring a 100\% evaluation budget.
In contrast, our MaxMin variant operates purely on the feature space and is
thus usable under a true fixed-budget constraint.

\subsection{Positioning Against the Knee Set}

\paragraph{Transfer and active learning.}
Jamshidi et al.~\cite{jamshidi2018learning} study transfer learning across
environments for configurable-system performance prediction but do not
compare sampling strategies head-to-head for a fixed initial budget.
Zuluaga et al.~\cite{zuluaga2013active} propose $\epsilon$-PAL, an active
learning procedure for Pareto-front identification, but target
Gaussian-process surrogates on continuous benchmarks rather than tabular SE
data.

\paragraph{Sequential model-based optimisation.}
Nair et al.~\cite{nair2018finding} introduce FLASH for configuration-space
search, which uses sequential evaluations guided by a surrogate, but the
initial random seed budget is fixed and no comparison of seed-sampling
strategies is made.
Our paper specifically isolates the initial-sampling question that such
sequential methods leave unanswered.

\paragraph{Diversity vs.\ evolutionary search.}
Chen et al.~\cite{chen2019sampling} compare SWAY to evolutionary algorithms
(NSGA-II, IBEA) on SE configuration benchmarks.
Their conclusion---that a simple diversity heuristic can rival much more
complex evolutionary machinery---motivates our inclusion of MaxMin.
Our contribution relative to SWAY is two-fold:
(1)~we enforce a \emph{true} evaluation budget (SWAY does not), and
(2)~we measure surrogate-recovered Pareto recall rather than the raw
dominance of the evaluated set.

\paragraph{Performance prediction.}
Siegmund et al.~\cite{siegmund2012predicting} and Guo et
al.~\cite{guo2018data} predict the performance of configurable systems via
supervised learning, but their setting is \emph{single-objective} (one
performance scalar per configuration) and does not involve Pareto-front
recovery.
They establish that tabular surrogates are a viable technology for SE data;
we extend that line of work to the multi-objective Pareto-recovery problem.

\paragraph{Summary.}
Returning to Figure~\ref{fig:venn}, each of the five criteria is partially
covered by prior work, but none of the 17~knee-set papers covers all five
simultaneously.
This paper is, to our knowledge, the first to compare multiple sampling
strategies for a multi-objective, surrogate-assisted, tabular-SE setting
under fixed absolute budgets.

\subsection{Origin of Benchmark Data}

All SE datasets are drawn from the MOOT repository~\cite{moot2025}---a
collection of real multi-objective optimization tasks organised around
software engineering benchmarks---supplemented with general tabular datasets
from diverse domains (financial, retail, health, automotive) to probe the
boundary of applicability beyond SE.
Objectives in MOOT datasets are annotated with \texttt{+}/\texttt{-} suffixes
denoting maximisation/minimisation directions, enabling automated multi-output
surrogate training.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Variables and Metrics}
\label{sec:variables}

\paragraph{Independent variables.}
Sampling strategy $s$: Random, Stratified, Clustering, or Diversity (MaxMin).
Fixed evaluation budget $n \in \{50, 100, 200, 250\}$ configurations.

\paragraph{Primary dependent variable: Pareto Recall.}
\begin{equation}
\text{Recall} = \frac{|PF_{\text{true}} \cap PF_{\text{pred}}|}{|PF_{\text{true}}|}
\end{equation}
Recall measures the fraction of truly Pareto-optimal configurations identified
by the surrogate's predicted front.
A value of 1.0 indicates perfect recovery; 0.0 means no true Pareto point
was found.
In our experiments the maximum observed grand-median is 0.275
(Diversity at $n=250$); the small absolute scale is a consequence of large
search spaces with proportionally small Pareto fronts (1--25\% of rows).
Recall is preferred over accuracy because the Pareto front is a small, critical
subset of the space---correct identification of non-Pareto points provides no
optimization value.

\paragraph{Secondary metrics.}
\textbf{Pareto Precision} ($|PF_{\text{true}} \cap PF_{\text{pred}}|/|PF_{\text{pred}}|$)
measures the purity of the predicted front.
\textbf{Inverted Generational Distance (IGD)} measures the average minimum
distance from each true Pareto point to the nearest predicted Pareto point;
lower is better.
\textbf{Hypervolume difference (HV-diff)} is the relative difference in
hypervolume between the true and predicted fronts.

We report median (not mean) recall across repeats and datasets.
Median is more robust to degenerate Pareto fronts (e.g., a dataset where
99\% of points are Pareto-optimal inflates mean recall without meaningful
signal).

\subsection{Dataset Selection}
\label{sec:datasets}

We selected 50 tabular multi-objective datasets sourced from two origins:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{MOOT benchmark~\cite{moot2025}}: datasets covering
  software process models (\texttt{pom3b/c/d}, \texttt{nasa93dem},
  \texttt{coc1000}), XOMO system simulation variants
  (\texttt{xomo\_ground/flight/osp/osp2}), software configuration spaces
  (15~\texttt{SS-*} datasets from the configuration landscape study, plus
  \texttt{Scrum1k/10k}, \texttt{FFM-250/500-SAT}, and four
  \texttt{FM-500-*} variants), and HPO health repository
  datasets (\texttt{Health-Issues0/1}, \texttt{Health-Commits0}).

  \item \textbf{General tabular sources}: automotive, financial, marketing,
  and human-resource datasets used to verify that findings are not
  restricted to SE benchmarks.
\end{enumerate}

Datasets were included if they had at least two objective columns (identified
by \texttt{+}/\texttt{-} suffix convention) and at least $n + 10$ rows for
the smallest applicable budget level.

\paragraph{Choice of budget levels.}
The four budget levels ($n = 50$, $100$, $200$, $250$) were chosen to span the
practically relevant range: 50 is the smallest budget at which a
100-tree Random Forest can be reliably trained on tabular data; 250
represents a generous but still frugal budget that represents about 1--3\%
of most datasets in our collection.
The minimum test set size of 10 configurations ensures that evaluation metrics
are computed on a meaningful hold-out.

\subsection{Sampling Strategies}
\label{sec:strategies}

All four strategies share the same interface: given feature matrix $X$
(numerical, after label-encoding), objective matrix $Y$, budget $n$, and a
random seed, return $n$ row indices.

\paragraph{Random.}
Uniform sampling without replacement from all row indices.
Serves as the baseline against which all other strategies are compared.

\paragraph{Stratified.}
The first objective column is divided into $\lceil\sqrt{n}\rceil$
equal-frequency bins; rows are sampled proportionally from each bin.
Ensures coverage of the range of the primary objective but ignores feature
space structure.

\paragraph{Clustering.}
$k$-Means is run with $k = n$ on the MinMax-normalized feature space;
the row closest to each centroid is selected.
Ensures spatial coverage of the feature space by construction; produces a
representative, space-filling sample.

\paragraph{Diversity (MaxMin).}
Greedy iterative selection: the first point is chosen at random; each
subsequent point maximizes the minimum Euclidean distance to the already-selected
set~\cite{sayyad2013value,chen2019sampling}.
Maximizes pairwise diversity in the feature space and is known to provide
good surrogate coverage in discrete spaces.

\subsection{Surrogate Model}

We use scikit-learn's multi-output \texttt{RandomForestRegressor}
with 100~trees and \texttt{n\_jobs=-1}~\cite{pedregosa2011scikit}.
The same model type is used for all datasets and strategies to isolate the
effect of the sampling strategy.
The surrogate is trained on the $n$ sampled configurations (with their true
objective values) and used to predict objectives for all remaining rows.
Predicted and true objectives are merged to form a complete objective matrix;
the Pareto front is then computed on the merged matrix using a standard
dominance check.

Random Forest was chosen over Gaussian processes or neural networks because
it (a)~handles discrete and mixed-type tabular data natively without tuning,
(b)~scales robustly from 93 to 20,000 rows, and (c)~has shown competitive
surrogate quality on SE configuration benchmarks in prior work~\cite{nair2018finding}.

\subsection{Experimental Pipeline}
\label{sec:pipeline}

For each (dataset, method, budget, repeat) tuple:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textit{Pre-process}: label-encode categorical features; impute missing
  values with per-column medians; normalize all objectives to $[0, 1]$ and
  flip signs so all become ``minimize.''

  \item \textit{Ground truth}: compute the true Pareto front $PF_{\text{true}}$
  on all rows using pairwise Pareto dominance.

  \item \textit{Sample}: select $n$ rows using the chosen strategy.

  \item \textit{Train}: fit the Random Forest surrogate on the selected rows.

  \item \textit{Predict}: predict objectives for all remaining rows; merge with
  training labels to form a full $N \times |obj|$ predicted matrix.

  \item \textit{Evaluate}: compute $PF_{\text{pred}}$ from predictions; record
  Recall, Precision, IGD, and HV-diff.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Each (dataset, method, budget) triple is repeated 10 times with
independent random seeds to account for variance in the stochastic strategies.
The full design has $4\;\text{strategies} \times 4\;\text{budgets} \times
50\;\text{datasets} \times 10\;\text{repeats} = 8{,}000$ nominal runs;
360 of these are skipped on the smallest datasets at the largest budgets
(where $n+10$ exceeds the row count), yielding \textbf{7{,}640 completed
experiments}.

\subsection{Data Analysis}

For each (dataset, budget) pair, the method with the highest \emph{median
recall across 10 repeats} is declared the winner (\textbf{win count} metric).
Global summaries use the median across all repeats \emph{and} all datasets
(grand median).
We use median throughout to suppress the influence of degenerate datasets and
outlier repetitions.

To assess whether differences are practically meaningful, we report the
Diversity vs.\ Random recall multiplier, following the comparison style of
Chen et al.~\cite{chen2019sampling}.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Experimental Rig}
\label{sec:rig}

All experiments were executed on a standard development laptop
(Intel Core i7, 16\,GB RAM) running Windows~11.
The software stack comprised Python~3.10, scikit-learn~1.3, NumPy~1.24,
and pandas~2.0; exact version pins are provided in the replication package.
Each (dataset, method, budget, repeat) tuple was run as an independent
invocation with no cross-tuple parallelism, ensuring a clean memory state
between runs.
Outputs were appended to per-dataset CSV files and subsequently merged by
\texttt{merge\_results.py}.

Total wall-clock runtime was approximately 18~hours across two overnight
sessions.
The bottleneck was the Diversity (MaxMin) strategy on the largest datasets
($\geq$10,000 rows), where iterative distance maximisation over the full
feature matrix dominates runtime.
Random seeds are fixed per repeat index: repeat $r$ uses seed $r$ for all
stochastic operations (initial point in MaxMin, $k$-Means initialisation, and
train/test splitting), so results for the same repeat index are directly
comparable across methods.

\subsection{What Was Seen}
\label{sec:whatseen}

Across all 7,640 experiments, two headline patterns emerged on the primary
recall metric; the secondary metrics tell a closely related but slightly
cleaner story (Diversity-dominant), discussed at the end of this subsection.

\textbf{Pattern~1---Strategy crossover (recall).}
At the smallest budget ($n=50$), \emph{Clustering} leads on recall: 28 of 50
dataset wins by median recall and median Pareto Recall of 0.093, which is
22\% above Diversity (0.077) and 63\% above Random (0.057).
For every larger budget ($n = 100, 200, 250$), \emph{Diversity} overtakes
Clustering in aggregate median recall.
The margin grows monotonically: at $n=250$, Diversity (0.275) exceeds
Clustering (0.194) by 42\% and Random (0.178) by 54\%.

\textbf{Pattern~2---Stratified and Random lag consistently.}
Stratified sampling never leads in aggregate median recall at any budget.
Random is competitive only in win count at $n=100$ (7 wins) but always trails
Diversity in median recall.
Neither objective-axis binning nor pure randomness exploits configuration-space
structure as effectively as spatial or diversity-based selection.

Both patterns hold with no meaningful exceptions across dataset sizes
(93--20,000 rows), objective counts (2--8), and all six application domains

\subsection{RQ2: Does the Optimal Strategy Change With Budget?}
\label{sec:rq2}

We examine the crossover between strategies as $n$ grows.
In median recall, Diversity overtakes Clustering between $n=50$ and $n=100$:
Diversity rises from 0.077 to 0.138 (+79\%), while Clustering rises
from 0.093 to 0.114 (+23\%).
Thereafter, the gap widens: at $n=250$, Diversity (0.275) exceeds
Clustering (0.194) by 42\%.

The win-count perspective nuances this slightly: Clustering still wins the
most individual dataset battles at $n=100$
(Clustering 24 vs.\ Diversity 13).
This means Clustering is marginally best on \emph{more} datasets, but Diversity
achieves larger margins on the datasets where it wins---driving the higher
aggregate median.
By $n=200$ and $n=250$, Diversity leads in both win counts and aggregate
median simultaneously.

The crossover is explained by the geometry of small vs.\ large samples:
at $n=50$, the $k$-Means partitioning enforces structured coverage that the
greedy MaxMin walk cannot achieve with only 50 points; as $n$ grows, MaxMin's
uniform spread of points across the feature space increasingly captures the
diverse regions needed to train an accurate surrogate.

  There is a strategy crossover between $n=50$ and $n=100$.
  \textbf{Use Clustering for budgets up to 50 evaluations; switch to
  Diversity for 100 or more evaluations.}%
}

\subsection{Evaluation: Rigor of Validation Measures}
\label{sec:evaluation}

\paragraph{Primary metric: Pareto Recall.}
Pareto Recall---the fraction of truly Pareto-optimal configurations recovered
by the surrogate's predicted front---is the most directly relevant measure for
decision-makers who need the \emph{true} best trade-off options.
A practitioner using the predicted front suffers most when a true Pareto point
is missing (false negative), not when a predicted front point is sub-optimal
(false positive).
Recall captures this asymmetry; precision does not.

We use Recall rather than $F_1$ because $|PF_\text{true}|$ is fixed by the
dataset while $|PF_\text{pred}|$ varies with the surrogate's output.
Optimising $F_1$ could reward surrogates that predict over-large fronts at the
cost of purity; optimising Recall alone reflects the decision-maker's concern.

\paragraph{Reporting median, not mean.}
We aggregate via \emph{median} across repeats and datasets.
Pareto front size varies from $<$1\% to 25\% of rows across our benchmark.
Datasets with large fronts produce inflated raw recall for all methods,
skewing the mean; median is robust to such outliers.

\paragraph{Secondary metrics as cross-checks.}
Three additional metrics were recorded alongside Recall (Table~\ref{tab:secondary}):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Pareto Precision}
  ($|PF_\text{true} \cap PF_\text{pred}|/|PF_\text{pred}|$):
  purity of the predicted front.
  Diversity is best at every budget.
  \item \textbf{Inverted Generational Distance (IGD)}: average minimum
  distance from each true Pareto point to the nearest predicted point
  (lower is better).
  Diversity is best at $n\geq100$; Stratified is marginally lower at $n=50$.
  \item \textbf{Hypervolume difference (HV-diff)}: relative shortfall in the
  dominated hypervolume; Diversity is best at every budget.
\end{itemize}
Across precision, IGD, and HV-diff, Diversity is the dominant strategy at
every budget level---including $n=50$, where Clustering wins on recall.
The one local exception is IGD at $n=50$, where Stratified is marginally
lower than Diversity (0.084 vs.\ 0.089).
No metric supports Random or Stratified as the best strategy at any budget,
reducing the risk that our headline finding is an artefact of metric choice.

\paragraph{Statistical rigour.}
We summarise results via win counts and medians across 50 independent datasets.
The empirical dominance margins are large (28 wins at $n=50$; a 42\% Recall
advantage at $n=250$), making formal significance tests unnecessary to
establish the finding.
A future study with a larger benchmark ($\geq$100 datasets) may benefit from
non-parametric tests such as Wilcoxon signed-rank or Scott-Knott effect sizes.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Results and Implications}
\label{sec:implications}

\paragraph{Fixed vs.\ percentage-based budgets.}
An earlier pilot of this study used percentage-based budgets (5\%--30\%) on
6~datasets.
Percentage budgets systematically favor small datasets---5\% of 100 rows
is 5 evaluations, while 5\% of 20,000 rows is 1,000---masking the crossover
identified here.
Fixed absolute budgets expose the true geometry of the sampling problem and
enable fair cross-dataset comparison.

\paragraph{Why not Stratified?}
Stratified sampling bins only the \emph{first} objective, ignoring the rest
of the objective space and the feature space.
This is a known limitation of single-axis stratification.
A multi-axis stratified approach~\cite{jamshidi2018learning} might improve
performance but at additional algorithmic complexity.

\paragraph{The role of the surrogate model.}
We use a single surrogate (Random Forest) to isolate sampling effects.
Prior work~\cite{nair2018finding} shows Random Forests are competitive for
SE configuration prediction.
Repeating this study with Gaussian process surrogates---which may better
quantify uncertainty---is a direction for future work.

\paragraph{Practical guidance.}
The crossover finding translates into a simple decision rule for the
practitioner whose primary concern is \emph{not missing} true Pareto points
(i.e., recall):
\emph{use Clustering for budgets $\leq 50$; use Diversity (MaxMin) for
budgets $\geq 100$.}
For practitioners who instead weight Precision, IGD, or HV-diff more
heavily---e.g., when each predicted Pareto point will be re-evaluated and
false positives are costly---\emph{Diversity is the safer default at every
budget}, including $n=50$.
Either rule is actionable before any data are collected because it depends
only on the practitioner's evaluation budget and objective, not on dataset
size or landscape.

\subsection{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}

\textit{Internal}: A possible confound is that $k$-Means in Clustering uses a
random initialisation; we use $n\_\text{init}=5$ and fix seeds to mitigate
this.
Diversity (MaxMin) also starts from a randomly chosen seed point; the same
seed-per-repeat scheme ensures fair comparison across methods.

\textit{External}: All datasets are tabular discrete spaces.
Continuous benchmark functions (DTLZ, ZDT) or real-world engineering
optimisation problems may behave differently under the same strategies.
Results may not transfer to settings with continuous or very high-dimensional
($>$100 features) input spaces.

\textit{Construct}: We use Pareto Recall as the primary metric because it
directly measures decision-relevant recovery.
Precision, IGD, and HV-diff show that Diversity dominates the secondary
metrics at nearly every budget (see \S\ref{sec:evaluation} and
Table~\ref{tab:secondary}); the only metric on which Clustering leads is
recall at $n=50$, and the only secondary cell where any other method leads
Diversity is Stratified on IGD at $n=50$ (0.084 vs.\ 0.089).
A reader who weights precision or hypervolume above recall would therefore
read our results as ``Diversity at every budget'' rather than as a
budget-dependent crossover.
We report both views to make this metric sensitivity explicit.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:futurework}

This study fixes the surrogate type (Random Forest) to isolate sampling
effects; several natural extensions follow.

\paragraph{Alternative surrogates.}
\textbf{Gaussian process (GP)} surrogates provide principled uncertainty
estimates that could drive active sampling (evaluate where the GP is most
uncertain), potentially shifting the crossover point.
\textbf{Neural network surrogates} may benefit differently from clustered
vs.\ diverse training data, especially on larger datasets.
Whether the Clustering-to-Diversity crossover is surrogate-agnostic or a
Random Forest artefact is an open question.

\paragraph{Adaptive and hybrid strategies.}
A combined strategy---Clustering for the first 50 evaluations, then MaxMin
for additional ones---might capture the best of both worlds across the full
budget range.
Alternatively, an active learning loop that selects each new evaluation based
on surrogate uncertainty could outperform any fixed sampling scheme.

\paragraph{Continuous and high-dimensional spaces.}
The current benchmark is restricted to tabular discrete spaces.
Extending the comparison to continuous benchmark functions (DTLZ, ZDT) and
real engineering problems would test whether the crossover is a general
property of surrogate-assisted MOO or specific to discrete configuration
spaces.

\paragraph{Formal statistical testing.}
A larger benchmark ($\geq$100 datasets) would permit non-parametric tests
(Wilcoxon signed-rank, Scott-Knott effect sizes) to provide statistical
guarantees alongside the empirical findings reported here.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We conducted the largest empirical study of fixed-budget sampling strategies
for surrogate-assisted multi-objective optimization: 50 diverse tabular datasets,
4 strategies, 4 budget levels, and 10 repeats per configuration (7,640
experiments).

Our central finding is a \textbf{budget-dependent crossover on Pareto
Recall}:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item At $n=50$ evaluations, \textbf{Clustering} recovers the most true
  Pareto points (28/50 dataset wins by median recall, median recall 0.093).
  \item At $n \geq 100$ evaluations, \textbf{Diversity (MaxMin)} is best
  on recall and its advantage grows monotonically---reaching 0.275 median
  recall at $n=250$, 42\% above Clustering and 54\% above Random.
  \item On the secondary metrics (Precision, IGD, HV-diff), Diversity is
  the best strategy at nearly every budget---only Stratified marginally
  beats it on IGD at $n=50$ (0.084 vs.\ 0.089).
\end{itemize}

This pattern is robust across dataset sizes, objective counts, and
application domains.
Practitioners optimising recall on tight budgets should use Clustering up to
$n=50$ and switch to Diversity by $n=100$; practitioners optimising
precision, IGD, or hypervolume should use Diversity at every budget.
